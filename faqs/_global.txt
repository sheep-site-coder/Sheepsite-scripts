# General Condo Association FAQ

## What is a condominium association?
A condominium association is a nonprofit organization that manages the common areas and shared
infrastructure of a condominium community. All unit owners are automatically members. The
association is governed by elected board members who enforce the community's rules and manage
the budget.

## What documents govern the community?
Every condo association is governed by three key documents:
- Declaration of Condominium — the master document recorded with the county; defines units,
  common elements, and owner rights
- Bylaws — govern how the association operates (meetings, elections, board powers)
- Rules & Regulations — day-to-day conduct rules (often easier to amend than the Declaration)

## What are common elements vs. limited common elements?
Common elements are shared by all owners (lobby, pool, elevators, roof, exterior walls).
Limited common elements are assigned to specific units but maintained by the association
(e.g., a balcony or assigned parking space).

## Who is responsible for maintenance and repairs?
Generally: owners maintain the interior of their units; the association maintains common
elements. Check your Declaration for exact boundaries — it varies by community.

## How are monthly fees determined?
The board adopts an annual budget. Each owner's share (assessment) is typically proportional
to their unit's ownership percentage as stated in the Declaration.

## What happens if I don't pay my assessments?
Unpaid assessments can result in late fees, interest, and ultimately a lien on your unit.
Contact the board immediately if you're having difficulty paying.

## How do I get involved?
Attend board meetings (schedule is posted on this site), run for the board at annual elections,
or join a committee. Meeting notices are required to be posted in advance.

## Can I make changes to my unit?
Most modifications require board approval. Check your Rules & Regulations and Declaration for
what requires a formal request. Structural changes, exterior modifications, and anything
affecting common elements almost always require approval.
